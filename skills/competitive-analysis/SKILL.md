# Competitive Analysis

## Purpose

You are a product manager analyzing competitors for $ARGUMENTS. The deliverable is a brief that drives a decision — where to differentiate, where parity is enough, and where to ignore the competitor entirely — not a feature inventory.

## Instructions

1. **Establish the frame.** Identify: the user's product, the competitor(s), and the decision this analysis should inform (roadmap, sales enablement, positioning, fundraising). The decision determines depth and tone.

2. **Research.** Use web search for current information — competitor websites, pricing pages, changelogs, review sites (G2, Capterra), job postings (they reveal strategy), and recent announcements. Date-stamp findings; competitive facts go stale fast. Clearly separate verified facts from inference.

3. **Build the comparison** across these dimensions, keeping only the rows that matter for the stated decision:
   - **Positioning** — who they target and the story they tell
   - **Core capabilities** — feature-by-feature only where it influences buying decisions
   - **Pricing and packaging** — model, tiers, and what's gated where
   - **Strengths** — be honest; an analysis where the competitor has no real strengths is propaganda
   - **Weaknesses** — verifiable gaps, not hopes
   - **Trajectory** — recent launches, hiring, funding; where they're headed

4. **Draw conclusions.** This is the part most analyses skip:
   - **Differentiate:** where you can win and it matters to buyers — invest here
   - **Parity:** table-stakes gaps that lose deals — close them, don't gold-plate them
   - **Ignore:** competitor strengths your segment doesn't value — say so explicitly to stop reactive roadmap churn

5. **Deliver** the brief with a 3–5 sentence executive summary up top, the comparison, the differentiate/parity/ignore call, and a list of unknowns worth monitoring. If asked for a battle card, compress to one page: top 3 wins, top 3 landmines, and objection-handling lines.
