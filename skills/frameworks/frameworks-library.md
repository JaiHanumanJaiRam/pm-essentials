# Framework Library

Grouped by the problem type they serve. "Best when" tells you the situation that makes the framework earn its keep; "Skip when" is the common misfit.

## Direction — where to play, what to bet on

- **Playing to Win (Lafley/Martin)** — Five cascading choices: winning aspiration, where to play, how to win, capabilities, management systems. *Best when:* setting or pressure-testing product/company strategy; exposes "strategies" that are really just goals. *Skip when:* the question is tactical.
- **Porter's Five Forces** — Industry attractiveness via supplier power, buyer power, new entrants, substitutes, rivalry. *Best when:* entering a market or explaining margin pressure. *Skip when:* analyzing a single competitor (use a battle card).
- **7 Powers (Helmer)** — Seven durable moats: scale economies, network economies, counter-positioning, switching costs, branding, cornered resource, process power. *Best when:* asking "what stops this from being copied?" *Skip when:* pre-product-market-fit.
- **Blue Ocean / ERRC grid** — Eliminate, Reduce, Raise, Create factors vs. industry standard to escape head-to-head competition. *Best when:* a commoditized category where everyone competes on the same factors. *Skip when:* the category is young and uncontested anyway.
- **Ansoff Matrix** — Growth options: existing/new products × existing/new markets. *Best when:* choosing among growth bets at portfolio level. *Skip when:* you've already committed to the quadrant.
- **SWOT** — Strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats. *Best when:* a fast shared snapshot before deeper work; pair each item with an implied action or it's a brainstorm dump. *Skip when:* rigor matters more than speed.
- **PESTLE** — Political, economic, social, technological, legal, environmental scan. *Best when:* regulated industries or geographic expansion. *Skip when:* macro factors don't differ across your options.
- **3C (Ohmae)** — Customer, Company, Competitor triangulation. *Best when:* a quick positioning sanity check. *Skip when:* you need depth on any one C.
- **TAM/SAM/SOM** — Market sizing from total to obtainable. *Best when:* justifying investment or fundraising; always build bottom-up as the cross-check. *Skip when:* the real question is "will anyone use this," not "how many."

## Selection — choosing among options, ordering work

- **RICE** — Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort. *Best when:* feature backlogs with real usage data. *Skip when:* no data — scores become laundered gut feel.
- **ICE** — Impact, Confidence, Ease. *Best when:* early-stage ideas, growth experiment queues. *Skip when:* stakes are high enough to deserve real analysis per item.
- **Kano Model** — Classifies features as basic expectations, performance, or delighters. *Best when:* deciding between table-stakes parity and differentiation. *Skip when:* you can't survey or interview users.
- **Opportunity scoring (importance vs. satisfaction)** — Find what users rate important but unsatisfying. *Best when:* mining survey data for underserved needs. *Skip when:* users can't articulate the need (new categories).
- **MoSCoW** — Must/Should/Could/Won't. *Best when:* scoping a release with stakeholders in the room. *Skip when:* everything lands in "Must" — then use forced ranking.
- **Weighted scoring** — Explicit criteria with weights. *Best when:* strategic criteria genuinely differ in importance and you can defend the weights. *Skip when:* weights would be invented after the preferred answer.
- **Eisenhower Matrix** — Urgent/important 2×2. *Best when:* personal or team workload triage. *Skip when:* the items are projects needing real prioritization.

## Diagnosis — something is wrong, cause unclear

- **Issue tree (MECE decomposition)** — Break the problem into mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive branches; size each. The consultant's workhorse. *Best when:* a metric is down or a goal is missed and causes are tangled. *Skip when:* the cause is already known — go fix it.
- **Hypothesis-driven problem solving** — State the answer first, then design the minimum analysis to confirm or kill it. *Best when:* time-boxed diagnosis; prevents boiling the ocean. *Skip when:* you have no basis for a hypothesis yet (do discovery first).
- **5 Whys** — Ask why repeatedly to move from symptom to root cause. *Best when:* a specific incident or failure. *Skip when:* multiple interacting causes — it forces a single chain.
- **Funnel analysis** — Decompose a flow into steps and find the biggest drop relative to benchmark. *Best when:* conversion, activation, or onboarding problems. *Skip when:* the product loop isn't linear.
- **80/20 (Pareto)** — Find the small share of causes driving most of the effect. *Best when:* support tickets, churn reasons, revenue concentration. *Skip when:* the distribution is actually flat.
- **Fishbone (Ishikawa)** — Categorized cause brainstorm (people, process, tech, environment...). *Best when:* a group needs to surface causes without anchoring on one. *Skip when:* working alone — an issue tree is sharper.

## Discovery — understanding users and needs

- **Jobs to be Done (JTBD)** — Users "hire" products to make progress in a circumstance; study the job, not the demographic. *Best when:* innovating beyond feature requests, or sales/churn interviews. *Skip when:* the job is obvious and the question is execution.
- **Opportunity Solution Tree (Teresa Torres)** — Outcome → opportunities → solutions → experiments, as a living tree. *Best when:* connecting continuous discovery to a metric; keeps solutions tied to evidence. *Skip when:* one-off decisions.
- **Double Diamond** — Diverge/converge on problem, then diverge/converge on solution. *Best when:* teams jumping to solutions before agreeing on the problem. *Skip when:* the problem is already well-defined.
- **Persona / segment grid** — Archetypes with goals, contexts, and behaviors. *Best when:* aligning a team on who you serve and who you don't. *Skip when:* personas would be fiction — too little research.
- **Empathy map** — What the user says, thinks, does, feels. *Best when:* synthesizing interview notes quickly. *Skip when:* you need quantitative confidence.
- **10-Star Experience (Chesky/Airbnb)** — Imagine what each tier of experience looks like from 1-star (disaster) through 5-star (expected) up to 10 or 11 stars (absurdly magical, physically impossible to scale). The gap between 5-star and 10-star is where differentiation hides. *Best when:* designing onboarding, key customer touchpoints, or any moment where delight drives word-of-mouth; forces teams past "good enough." *Skip when:* the product is pure utility with no experience layer — users don't tell stories about their tax software.

## Growth — acquisition, retention, monetization

- **AARRR (Pirate Metrics)** — Acquisition, activation, retention, referral, revenue. *Best when:* locating the weakest stage of the funnel to focus on. *Skip when:* growth is loop-driven rather than funnel-driven.
- **Growth loops** — Map self-reinforcing loops (content, viral, sales) where output feeds input. *Best when:* designing compounding growth instead of paid-channel arithmetic. *Skip when:* the product has no plausible loop — be honest.
- **North Star framework** — One metric capturing delivered value, plus 3–5 input metrics teams can move. *Best when:* aligning multiple teams on what "growth" means. *Skip when:* a single metric would distort behavior (early B2B, marketplaces with two sides — use paired metrics).
- **Retention curve analysis** — Plot cohort retention; flat tail = product-market fit signal, decay to zero = leaky bucket. *Best when:* deciding whether to pour fuel on acquisition. *Skip when:* too few cohorts to read.
- **Race Car Framework (Lenny/Casey Winters)** — Growth engine vs. turbo boosts vs. lubricants vs. fuel. *Best when:* explaining why one-off launches don't compound. *Skip when:* you need numbers, not metaphor.
- **Value Ladder** — Map the sequence of offers from free/low-commitment to premium/high-commitment; each rung builds trust and LTV. *Best when:* monetization is stalled because users aren't converting to higher tiers — identifies the missing bridge. *Skip when:* the business is single-product with no upsell path.
- **Habit Loop (Hooked — Nir Eyal)** — Trigger → action → variable reward → investment; each cycle strengthens the next. *Best when:* designing for engagement, notification strategy, or reducing churn through habit formation. *Skip when:* the product's value is delivered once (one-time purchase, filing a form) — forced engagement loops feel dark.

## Decision risk — big, hard-to-reverse calls

- **Pre-mortem (Tigers / Paper Tigers / Elephants)** — Assume failure, work backward; classify real risks, overblown worries, undiscussed concerns. *Best when:* before launch or commitment. *Skip when:* the decision is cheap to reverse — just ship.
- **One-way vs. two-way doors (Bezos)** — Classify reversibility first; spend analysis only on one-way doors. *Best when:* a team is over-analyzing a reversible call or under-analyzing an irreversible one. *Skip when:* never — this one's nearly free.
- **Expected value / decision tree** — Outcomes × probabilities × payoffs, compared across branches. *Best when:* options have quantifiable, asymmetric payoffs. *Skip when:* probabilities would be pure invention — use scenarios instead.
- **Inversion** — Ask "what would guarantee failure?" and avoid those things. *Best when:* stuck, or success criteria are fuzzy. *Skip when:* you already have a concrete risk list.
- **Second-order thinking** — "And then what?" — trace consequences of consequences, especially competitor and user adaptation. *Best when:* pricing changes, platform policy, incentive design. *Skip when:* first-order effects are still unknown.
- **10/10/10** — How will this look in 10 minutes, 10 months, 10 years? *Best when:* emotion or urgency is distorting a call. *Skip when:* the decision is purely analytical.

## Communication & alignment

- **Pyramid Principle (Minto)** — Lead with the answer, then grouped supporting arguments, then data. *Best when:* exec communication, memos, recommendations. *Skip when:* the audience needs to feel they reached the conclusion themselves — then guide, don't declare.
- **SCQA** — Situation, Complication, Question, Answer — a narrative opening that earns attention. *Best when:* framing a problem before pitching the solution. *Skip when:* the audience already agrees on the problem.
- **Stakeholder map (power × interest)** — Plot who can block or boost, and engagement strategy per quadrant. *Best when:* cross-functional initiatives with politics. *Skip when:* two stakeholders — just talk to them.
- **RACI** — Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed per workstream. *Best when:* ownership confusion is causing dropped balls. *Skip when:* it would formalize what a five-person team does naturally.
- **Working backwards (Amazon PR/FAQ)** — Write the launch press release and FAQ before building. *Best when:* forcing clarity on customer value early. *Skip when:* the work is internal/technical with no customer story.

## Metrics & goal-setting

- **OKRs** — Objectives (qualitative, inspiring) with key results (quantitative, verifiable). *Best when:* aligning teams on outcomes over output. *Skip when:* they'd just relabel the existing task list.
- **HEART (Google)** — Happiness, engagement, adoption, retention, task success — UX metrics per feature. *Best when:* measuring UX quality beyond usage counts. *Skip when:* business metrics are what's contested.
- **Input vs. output metrics** — Identify controllable inputs that drive the outcome metric; manage inputs, monitor outputs. *Best when:* teams are accountable for numbers they can't directly move. *Skip when:* the causal chain from input to output is unproven — test it first.
- **GQM (Goal-Question-Metric)** — Derive metrics from goals via the questions they must answer. *Best when:* designing a dashboard from scratch. *Skip when:* metrics are already mandated.
