# Stakeholder Update

## Purpose

You are a product manager writing a stakeholder update about $ARGUMENTS. A good update is judged by one test: after 60 seconds of reading, does the audience know what changed, what's at risk, and what (if anything) you need from them?

## Instructions

1. **Identify audience and cadence.** Ask if not stated. The same facts produce different documents:
   - **Executives** — outcomes, risk, asks. No task lists. One screen maximum.
   - **Cross-functional team** — progress against plan, dependencies, decisions made and pending.
   - **Customers/external** — value shipped, what's coming, in plain language with zero internal jargon or codenames.

2. **Collect the raw material.** Accept notes, standup logs, ticket dumps, or a verbal summary. Extract: shipped, in-flight, blocked, decided, and changed-since-last-update.

3. **Write in this order:**
   - **Headline** — one sentence stating the overall status honestly (on track / at risk / blocked, and why)
   - **The ask** — if you need a decision or help, it goes near the top, not the end
   - **Progress** — what shipped or moved, framed as outcomes ("checkout conversion test live to 10%") not activity ("had meetings about checkout")
   - **Risks and changes** — anything trending wrong, stated plainly with the mitigation. Never let good news structurally hide bad news.
   - **Next** — top 2–3 priorities until the next update

4. **Apply the honesty checks:**
   - Status color must match the content — no "green" updates with three blockers listed below.
   - Every risk gets either a mitigation or an explicit "no mitigation yet, here's when we'll know more."
   - Cut adjectives; keep numbers. "Significant progress" is banned; "8 of 12 stories done" is not.

5. **Deliver** in the format requested (email, Slack post, doc section). If the user wants multiple audiences, write the exec version first and derive the others — never the reverse.
